F1 Race Calendar Optimization



Every year, Formula One travels around the world to put on a racing spectacle. From Mexico to Japan, race locations span 5 continents and over 20 countries. While the fans are able to enjoy the racing from the sidelines or at home, the planning behind these races is a big challenge. Specifically, the task of moving around all these tracks, sometimes in just a few days during back-to-back race weekends, results in a logistical nightmare. The 2025 Formula One race calendar visits 24 tracks as seen below. Starting in Melbourne, Australia and ending in Abu Dhabi, UAE, the 2025 Formula One race calender will be 114,494 km long!

As you can see, this race calendar involves multiple trips across the world with some of the longest distances being from Singapore to Austin and Las Vegas to Lusail. There are many reasons why these large travel distances exist such as location climates, religious events, and track contracts. Furthermore, teams often travel back to thier home bases between races, adding even more travel to the already busy schedule. However, if the goal was just to visit every track on the calendar once, what would be the shortest path?


In this project, I aimed to answer that question by formulating and coding an optimization model that finds the shortest path among a given set of cities. I based this model off an existing optimization model to solve the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP). The TSP involves finding the shortest cycle among a set of cities that starts and ends at the same city. Variations of the TSP exist, one being the Freely-Open-Loop (FOL) TSP, which involves finding the shortest path among a set of cities that starts and ends at different cities.


Using the navigation buttons above, you can test the FOL TSP model I created under different city parameters and compare it to other heuristic models that solve the problem. The inspiration for this project came from a YouTube video I found where the Nearest Neighbor method was used to solve this problem.
